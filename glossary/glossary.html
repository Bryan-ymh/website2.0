A

AcquisitionWhere one company purchases most or all of another company's shares in order to take control (more than 50% ownership).

AppreciationAn increase in the value of an investment.

AskAsk price is the price at which the investor is willing to accept for selling a certain stock.

AssetAnything having commercial or exchange value that is owned by an individual, institution, or company.

Asset classA category of investments such as stocks, bonds, or cash equivalents.

Average annual total returnThe yearly average percentage increase or decrease in an investment's value that includes dividends, gains, and changes in share price.

B

Balance sheetBalance sheet lists out the assets, liabilities and the shareholder's equity of a company, and shows its financial status and the asset and liabilities allocation at a given point of time (such as end of year).

Basis pointOne-hundredth of one percent, or 0.01%. For example, 20 basis points equals to 0.20%. Investment expenses, interest rates, and yield differences among bonds are often expressed as basis points.

BidBid price is the price at which the investor is willing to pay for buying a certain stock.

BondThe debt instrument (or "IOU") of a corporation or government entity that promises to pay you a specified amount of interest for a specified time period, with principal to be repaid when the bond matures.

Bond fundA fund that invests primarily in bonds and other debt instruments.

Bond ratingA rating that is intended to indicate the credit quality of a bond, which considers the issuer's financial strength and the likelihood that it will repay the debt. Agencies such as Standard & Poor's, Moody's Investors Service, and Fitch issue ratings for different bonds. The ratings range from AAA (highly unlikely to default) to D (in default).

C

Capital gainAny gain realized from the sale of capital receipt.

Capital lossThe loss in the value of an investment, calculated by the difference between the purchase price and the net sale price.

Capitalization (Cap)The total market value of a company’s outstanding equity.

Cash flow statement Cash flow statement is a summary of the cash receipts and payment of a company over an accounting period, which classifies the cash receipts (i.e. cash inflows) and cash payments (i.e. cash outflows) of the company into returns on investments and servicing of finance, taxation, operating activities, investing activities and financing activities. It reflects a company's liquidity and solvency and presents changes in cash position of the company during that accounting period. Cash flow statement eliminates the long-term provisions and other allocations associated with accrual accounting, and depicts the historical cash generating or cash absorption mechanisms of an entity. In conjunction with profit & loss account and balance sheet, cash flow statement provides comprehensive financial information of a company.

ChangeNet change is the price difference between the current nominal price and the previous close price, displayed in price value and/or in percentage in general.

CommodityA basic good used in commerce that is interchangeable with other commodities of the same type. For example, a barrel of oil is basically the same product, regardless of the producer. Whereas for electronics merchandise, the quality and features of a given product may be different depending on the producer. The sale and purchase of commodities are usually carried out through futures contracts on exchanges where quantity and minimum quality of the commodity being traded is standardized.

Common stockSecurities that represent an ownership interest and give the investor voting rights in the issuing corporation.

Compound interestInterest earned not only on the original investment but on its accrued earnings as well.

Corporate bondA bond issued by a corporation, rather than by a government. The credit risk for a corporate bond is based on the repayment ability of the company (issuer).

Coupon rateThe yield the bond is paid on its issue date.

Credit riskThe risk that a bond issuer will default, meaning not repay principal or interest to the investor as promised. Credit risk is also known as “default risk.”

Current incomeAn investment income earned from interest, dividends, rent, and similar sources.

Current yield The current rate of return of an investment calculated by dividing its expected income payments by its current market price.

CustodianA person or entity (e.g. bank, trust company, or other organization) responsible for holding financial assets.

D

DefaultFailure to meet the legal obligations or conditions of a loan. For example, when a corporation fails to pay a bond which has matured.

DepreciationA decrease in the value of an investment.

DividendPayments made by a corporation to its shareholders from past and current earnings. The amount you receive is based on the amount of shares you own.

Dividends Per Share (DPS)Dividends Per Share (DPS) = Dividends / Issued Common Shares

Dollar-cost averagingInvesting a fixed amount of money in a specific investment at regular intervals, regardless of market conditions or prices. More shares are purchased when prices are low and fewer shares are purchased when prices are high. In a fluctuating market, the average cost per share is generally lower than the average price per share.
Advantages:
1. No emotions attached - Dollar-cost average means investing mechanically by buying a certain dollar amount of your preferred investment disregarding of how the price swings. See it as an opportunity to acquire more shares at a lower cost.
2. Avoids bad timing - By investing periodically you, you would end up losing lesser money during a market downturn. On the other hand you might miss out investing at just the right time before the market turns bullish. Disadvantages:
1. Good pick or bad pick? - You will have to identify good investments anyway. If your choice turns out to be a bad pick, you will be investing steadily into a losing investment.
2. Less flexibility - By adapting this passive approach you will not be responding to the changing environment, therefore it does not allow dynamic portfolio management.

Dow Jones Industrial Average (Dow or DJIA)A price-weighted index of 30 of the largest, most widely held U.S. stocks.

E

Earnings Per Share (EPS)Earnings Per Share (EPS) = Net Profit after Taxation / Issued Common Shares.

EquityThe ownership interest of shareholders in a corporation.

Equity fundA fund that invests primarily in equities.

Exchange-traded fund (ETF)A type of investment company whose shares trade on stock exchanges at prices determined by the market.

Exercise priceThe exercise price of a warrant is the pre-determined price at which the holder can exercise his right to buy or sell the underlying asset.

F

Face valueThe amount a bond issuer is required to repay investors on the bond’s maturity date. It is also known as par value.

Financial statementsFinancial statements provide useful financial information for investors and creditors to predict, compare and evaluate potential cash flow of a company. The financial statements comprise (i) Profit & Loss Account, (ii) Balance Sheet and (iii) Cash Flow Statement.

Fixed income fundA fund that invests primarily in bonds and other fixed income securities, often to provide shareholders with current income.

Fixed income securitiesInvestments with specified payment dates and amounts, primarily bonds that pay interest.

G

Global fundA fund that invests in stocks throughout the world.

Growth fund A fund that invests primarily in the stocks of companies with above-average risk in return for potentially above-average gains. These companies often pay small or no dividends and their stock prices tend to have the most ups and downs from day to day.

Growth stockThe stock of a firm whose earnings are generally growing faster than the economy or market norm. Investment risk with growth stock tends to be high.

H

Hang Seng Index (HSI)A market capitalization-weighted index of the largest companies that trade on the Hong Kong Exchange. Some of these companies include Tencent Holdings Limited, Sun Hung Kai Properties Limited, and AIA Group Limited.

HighDay high price is the highest nominal price of a stock within the trading hours of the day and is computed by the exchange.

I

Income fundA fund that invests in stocks throughout the world.

Income stock Common stock that pays out a relatively large portion of earnings as dividends, resulting in a high yield for investors.

IndexA benchmark against which to evaluate a fund’s performance.

Index fund: Bond A type of mutual fund or unit investment trust whose investment objective typically is to achieve approximately the same return as a particular bond index by investing in the bonds of the issuers included in the index. The fees and expenses of an index fund is usually lower than those of an actively managed fund because it is “passively” managed.

Index fund: Stock A type of mutual fund or unit investment trust whose investment objective typically is to achieve approximately the same return as a particular stock index by investing in the stocks of the issuers included in the index. The fees and expenses of an index fund is usually lower than those of an actively managed fund because it is “passively” managed.

Inflation A quantitative measure, presented in percentage of the rate at which the average price level of selected goods and services in an economy increases over a period of time. Inflation represents a decrease in the purchasing power of a nation’s currency. As prices increase, it will affect the cost of living for the general public, which then forces the country’s monetary authority to take appropriate measures to keep inflation within permissible limits and keep the economy running smoothly.

InterestAn amount charged or paid for borrowing or using money.

InvestmentA stock, bond, mutual fund, or other asset that offers you the potential of profitable returns.

L

Liabilities Liabilities mainly comprise Current Liabilities, long-term liabilities and minority interests. Current liabilties include accounts payable, notes payable, accrued expenses, short-term borrowings, current portion of long-term debt and lease obligations, etc. Long-term liabilities include long-term debt, convertible loans, etc.

Liquefiable assetsLiquefiable assets mainly comprise inter-bank deposits with maturity date within one month, HK Dollars or foreign currencies, gold, marketable securities and advances with maturity date within one month.

Lot sizeLot size of a stock is the minimum volume for an investor to buy or sell the stock.

Low Day low price is the lowest nominal price of a stock within the trading hours of the day and is computed by the exchange.

M

Market capitalizationThe value of a company’s outstanding stock, calculated by multiplying the current share price by the number of shares of stock owned by investors.

Market index A measurement of the performance of a specific “basket” of stocks, bonds, or other type of investment considered to represent a particular market or sector of the stock or markets or the economy.

Market value ratio Market value ratios are used for comparing the value of different companies. These ratios are not included in financial statements and can only be calculated from listed companies.

Maturity The date by which the issuer of a bond promises to repay the bond’s face value.

Mutual fund An investment that combines money from shareholders and invests it in numerous securities, including stocks, bonds, and short-term money market instruments. As open-ended investments, most mutual funds continuously offer shares to investors.

N

NASDAQAn American stock exchange in short for The National Association of Securities Dealers Automated Quotation. The NASDAQ composite index measures the performance of more than 3,000 U.S. and non-U.S. companies traded on the NASDAQ stock market.

Net Asset Value (NAV)The net value of an entity. It’s calculated as the total value of the entity’s assets minus the total value of its liabilities. The NAV presents the per share/unit price of the fund on a specific date or time, commonly used in the context of a mutual fund or an exchange-traded fund (ETF).

Net Profit MarginNet Profit Margin = Net Profit after Taxation / Turnover

New York Stock Exchange (NYSE)The oldest and largest stock exchange in the U.S., founded in 1792.

Nominal priceNominal price is the current price of a stock and is computed by the exchange during trading hours. It is not necessarily equal to the bid price, ask price or the final execution price.

O

Operating Profit Margin Operating Profit Margin = Operating Profit / Turnover

Operating activitiesOperating activities include cash receipts from customers, cash payments to suppliers and employees, and other payments for operating expenses.

P

PortfolioA collection of securities, such as stocks, bonds, or mutual fund shares, owned by an individual or an organization.

Previous closePrevious close price is the close price of the previous trading day.

Price-to-Earnings ratio (P/E ratio)The P/E ratio is the ratio used for valuing a company that measures its current share price relative to its per-share earnings (EPS). It is often used to determine the relative value of a company’s shares in a direct comparison.

PrincipalThe original amount (capital sum) invested, as distinguished from interest or profit.

Profit & loss account (Income statement)Profit & loss account, also called income statement, is a summary of a company's revenues, operation cost and expenses within an accounting period. It shows the profit and loss of the company in the accounting period and reflects the operation status.

R

ReturnThe profit or loss, earned or incurred, through investing.

Return on Investments (ROI)Returns on Investments and servicing of finance include cash receipts of interests and dividends, and cash payments for interests and dividends.

RiskThe possibility that an investment will not perform as anticipated. An acceptable degree of risk must be determined by you, with the understanding that the higher the expected return, the greater the risk factor. There are many different kinds of risk such as market, inflation, interest rate, liquidity, political, etc.

Risk tolerance Your ability or willingness to endure declines in the value of investments in exchange for a greater potential investment return.

S

S&P 500 stock indexA composite index of 500 large company stocks compiled by Standard & Poor’s Corporation that is used as a broad measure of U.S. stock market performance.

SecuritiesAssets, such as stocks, bonds, etc. that allow you to participate in earnings, distribution of property, or other assets of the corporation issuing the security.

Securities and Exchange Commission (SEC)The U.S. Securities and Exchange Commission (SEC) is an independent agency of the United States federal government. The SEC holds primary responsibility for enforcing the federal securities laws, proposing securities rules, and regulating the securities industry, the nation's stock and options exchanges, and other activities and organizations, including the electronic securities markets in the United States. It was formed in 1934.

Securities and Futures Commission (SFC)The SFC of Hong Kong is an independent statutory created by the government to regulate the securities and futures markets, to help protect investors, and to promote Hong Kong as an international financial centre. The SFC was created in 1989 in response of the stock market crash in 1987.

Share A representation of ownership in a corporation, mutual fund, or some other type of financial investment.

ShareholderAn investor who owns shares in a mutual fund or any other company.

Shareholders' equity Shareholders' equity mainly comprises share capital and reserves, and is equal to the difference between the assets and liabilities.

Small-cap stocks Stocks of companies with a smaller market capitalization. Small caps are often considered to offer more growth potential than large caps and mid-caps but with more risk.

Spread Spread is the smallest price unit of which the next bid price shall decrease or ask price shall increase. For example, if the current bid price of a stock is 109.00 and its bid spread is 0.500, this means that the next bid price down in the queue will be 108.500. On the other hand, if the current ask price of a stock is 109.500 and its ask spread is 0.500, this means the next ask price up in the ask queue will be 110.000.

Stock tradingWhen you decide to trade you issue a market order with your brokerage firm that signals you would like to purchase X number of shares in a stock, which means you are agreeing to purchase the stock at the price available when the order is executed. You may then decide to sell X number of shares of that stock at a price you agree on.

StocksShares of a corporation. It is also known as “equities.” They give you an ownership interest in the company that’s issuing the stock. Owners are usually entitled to receive dividends and vote on important company matters.

T

Ticker symbolAn abbreviation using letters and numbers assigned to securities and indexes to identify them.

Total return A measure of investment performance that takes into account the unrealized increase/decrease in the investment’s value during a specific time period, as well as the income generated by the investment during that period. It is usually expressed as a percentage of the initial investment.

Transaction A transaction of a stock is a trade executed by an investor who buys and another investor who sells the stock at a particular price and volume. In general, the transaction details include transaction time, transaction price, trade type and transaction volume.

U

Unit valueThe dollar value of each unit on a given date.

V

Volatility The amount and frequency of fluctuations in the price of a security, commodity, or a market within a specific time period. Generally, an investment with high volatility is said to have higher risk since there is an increased chance that the price of the security will have fallen when an investor wants to sell.

Y

YieldThe interest or dividend paid with respect to a security. Yield is usually expressed as a percentage of a security’s price. Some investment advisors include capital appreciation as part of the yield.
